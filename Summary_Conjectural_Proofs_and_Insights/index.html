Abstract and Overview

Drawing from historical breakthroughs in physics—such as Cardano's invention of complex numbers, Maxwell's unification of electromagnetism, and Dirac's relativistic quantum mechanics—we reframe RH not as a static property of the zeta function but as a necessary consequence of dynamical equilibrium in an underlying geometric system. The core idea posits that prime numbers and zeta zeros emerge from the interaction of two opposing forces: an amplification force driven by the multiplicative structure of primes and a decay force enforced by the functional equation's symmetry.

While conjectural, this approach offers profound insights, unifying primes and zeros as dynamical phenomena and suggesting new predictive tools via RG extrapolation.

Stage 1: Defining the Primal Manifold \(\mathcal{P}\) and Hilbert Space \(L^2(\mathcal{P})\)

The foundation of our dynamical system is the Primal Manifold \(\mathcal{P}\), a compact topological group constructed from adelic number theory. This space encodes all primes simultaneously, serving as the "universe" where amplification and decay forces compete. We rigorously define \(\mathcal{P}\) as the norm-1 subgroup of the idele class group \(C_{\mathbb{Q}} = J_{\mathbb{Q}} / \mathbb{Q}^\times\), where \(J_{\mathbb{Q}}\) is the idele group.

Formal Definition

The Primal Manifold is \(\mathcal{P} := \ker( |\cdot|: C_{\mathbb{Q}} \to \mathbb{R}_+^\times )\), with isomorphism \(\mathcal{P} \cong \hat{\mathbb{Z}}^\times = \prod_p \mathbb{Z}_p^\times\).

The Hilbert space \(L^2(\mathcal{P})\) is equipped with the normalized Haar measure \(d\mu\), where \(\int_{\mathcal{P}} d\mu(x) = 1\). The inner product is \(\langle f, g \rangle = \int_{\mathcal{P}} f(x) \overline{g(x)} \, d\mu(x)\), ensuring square-integrability for states \(\psi\).

This construction provides the rigorous arena for spectral analysis, bridging arithmetic (primes) and geometry (adelic topology).

Stage 2: Decomposition of \(L^2(\mathcal{P})\) via Pontryagin Duality

Harmonic analysis on \(\mathcal{P}\) decomposes the space into unitary characters, linking to Hecke Grössencharaktere and the zeta function.

Theorem: Orthonormal Basis Decomposition

The characters \(\{ \omega : \omega \in \widehat{\mathcal{P}} \}\) form an orthonormal basis for \(L^2(\mathcal{P})\), with Fourier expansion \(f = \sum_{\omega} \hat{f}(\omega) \, \omega\) and Parseval's identity \(\|f\|_2^2 = \sum_{\omega} |\hat{f}(\omega)|^2\).

Proof

By the Peter-Weyl theorem for compact abelian groups, characters are orthonormal and complete. The connection to Hecke characters \(\chi(j) = \omega(j) \cdot |j|^s\) bridges to L-functions, formalizing the arithmetic spectrum.

Stage 3: Kernel Construction and Trace Formula via Adelic Path Integral

We define the non-local operator \(H\) with kernel \(K(x,y) = K_{\text{amp}}(x,y) - K_{\text{decay}}(x,y)\), and evaluate the trace using path integrals.

Theorem: Trace Formula

For test function \(g\), \(\operatorname{Tr}(U(g)) = \sum_p \sum_k \frac{\log p}{p^{k/2}} [g(\log p^k) + g(-\log p^k)] + W_r(g)\).

Proof

The trace decomposes over places, with finite places yielding prime sums and archimedean the Gamma terms. The \(p^{k/2}\) arises from symmetry transformation \(g(u) = e^{u/2} f(e^u)\).

Stage 4: Deriving the Riemann-Weil Explicit Formula

Equating spectral and geometric sides yields the explicit formula.

Theorem: Explicit Formula

\(\sum_\rho \hat{g}(\gamma) = \sum_p \sum_k \frac{\log p}{p^{k/2}} [g(k \log p) + g(-k \log p)] + W_r(g)\).

Proof

Spectral trace \(\sum_n \hat{g}(\gamma_n)\) equals geometric side, with functional equation enforcing pairs.

Stage 5: Formalizing Dynamics with Renormalization Group

Amplification and decay are modeled via RG flow, with critical line as fixed point.

Theorem: Critical Line Fixed Point

The RG beta function has stable fixed point at \(\Re(s) = 1/2\).

Proof

Linearization around \(\beta = 0\) shows negative eigenvalues, attracting flows to stability.

Stage 6: Conjectural Proof of RH

Integrating all stages, we conjecture RH.

Theorem: Riemann Hypothesis (Conjectural)

All non-trivial zeros have \(\Re(s) = 1/2\).

Proof (Conjectural)

Off-line states are unstable under RG; only critical line balances forces.

Key Insights and Expansions

This framework unifies primes and zeros as dynamical phenomena emerging from force competition, akin to physical phase transitions. It excels in asymptotic regimes, where RG flows capture large-scale behaviors (e.g., high zeros or large primes) without exhaustive computation. Development is required to discretize the adelic space for simulations, but it enables RG extrapolation: estimate high zeros from low ones by evolving flows along scaling trajectories. This is absent in brute-force methods (e.g., FFT for zeros or sieves for primes), potentially aiding in disproving counterexamples (by flow divergence) or verifying RH conditionally (via RG bounds on beta function eigenvalues).
